Website Conversion Rate Optimization: The Practical Checklist

If your website gets traffic but not customers, the problem almost never lives in your ad spend or your keywords. It lives on the page itself. Website conversion rate optimization is the process of fixing what happens after someone arrives — the speed, the layout, the form, the trust signals — so more of that traffic turns into leads or sales.

Most sites lose visitors in the first few seconds, long before a headline or a discount code ever matters. The good news is that the fixes are concrete. This is a checklist, not a theory — built from what actually moves conversion numbers on real sites.

Why Websites Convert Poorly in the First Place

Low conversion rates are rarely one big mistake. They're usually a stack of small ones:

  • The page loads slowly, so a chunk of visitors leave before they see anything.
  • The mobile layout was an afterthought, bolted onto a desktop design.
  • There are five competing calls to action instead of one clear one.
  • The value proposition isn't visible until the visitor scrolls.
  • The contact form asks for more information than anyone wants to give up front.
  • There's no proof — no testimonials, no results, nothing that says "this works."

Each of these costs you visitors independently. Stack them together and you get a site that gets traffic but never turns it into revenue. That's exactly the pattern ARKA sees most often when a client comes in after working with a previous agency.

The Website Conversion Rate Optimization Checklist

1. Fix Load Speed First

A meaningful share of mobile visitors abandon a page that takes more than a few seconds to load, and every additional second of load time correlates with fewer conversions. This is the highest-leverage fix on this list because it affects 100% of your traffic, not just a segment. Sub-1 second load time should be the baseline, not the goal.

2. Design Mobile-First, Not Mobile-Adjusted

Most traffic to most sites is mobile. If your layout was designed for desktop and then "made responsive," it shows — cramped tap targets, text that requires zooming, forms that are painful to fill out with a thumb. Build for the phone screen first, then scale up.

3. Put One Clear Call to Action Above the Fold

A visitor should understand what you do and what to do next within about three seconds, without scrolling. If your homepage has a "Learn More," a "Get Started," a "Contact Us," and a newsletter signup all competing for attention, none of them win. Pick one primary action per page and make it visually obvious.

4. Make the Value Proposition Impossible to Miss

Above-the-fold content needs to answer "what is this and why should I care" instantly. Vague taglines and stock photography don't do that job. Specific, plain language does.

5. Cut Form Friction

Every field on a form is a reason for someone to stop filling it out. Ask for the minimum you need to follow up — usually name, email, and one qualifying question. You can collect the rest after the lead converts, not before.

6. Place Trust Signals Where the Doubt Happens

Testimonials, client logos, and results don't need to be everywhere — they need to be right where a visitor is hesitating. That's usually right before a form or a pricing decision. A specific, attributed testimonial does more work than a wall of five-star icons with no names attached.

7. Structure Pages for Scanning, Not Reading

Almost nobody reads a website top to bottom. They scan headlines, bold text, and images looking for relevance. Short paragraphs, clear subheadings, and visual hierarchy let a scanning visitor find what they need without giving up.

8. Remove Anything That Isn't Doing a Job

Extra navigation links, unrelated blog widgets, autoplay videos, and pop-ups that fire before a visitor has read anything — all of it adds friction. If an element on the page isn't building trust or moving someone toward the CTA, it's costing you conversions, not adding value.

9. Test on Real Devices, Not Just a Browser Window

A resized browser window is not the same as a real phone with a real connection speed. Load your own site on your own phone, on mobile data, and time how long it takes before you'd give up. That's the experience most of your visitors are having.

10. Monitor Performance, Don't Set and Forget

Conversion rate optimization isn't a one-time project. Ongoing performance monitoring catches the slow creep — a plugin that slows load time, a broken form field, a CTA that stopped rendering on one browser — before it quietly costs you leads for months.

Common Mistakes That Undo Good CRO Work

  • Testing before fixing the foundation. Running A/B tests on button colors while your load time is 6 seconds is testing on top of a broken base. Fix speed and structure first.
  • Adding more CTAs instead of clarifying one. More options usually means more hesitation, not more conversions.
  • Burying the value proposition below a hero image. A beautiful photo that pushes your message below the fold costs you the visitors who never scroll.
  • Treating trust signals as decoration. A generic "trusted by thousands" line does far less than one specific, attributed result.
  • Over-asking on forms. Every optional field you don't need is a visitor you didn't have to lose.
  • Ignoring mobile until it's an afterthought. If most of your traffic is mobile and your design was built for desktop first, you're optimizing for the wrong device.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a good website conversion rate?

It depends on your industry and traffic quality, but most B2B and SaaS sites convert somewhere between 1% and 3%. Anything above 5% is strong. Below 1% usually points to a structural problem — slow load times, unclear messaging, or too much friction between the visitor and the action you want them to take.

How long does it take to see results from CRO changes?

Some fixes show up almost immediately — cutting load time or simplifying a form changes behavior within days, once you have enough traffic to see the pattern. Rebuilding the site itself is a bigger swing. In the case above, the client saw the jump from 0.4% to 6.8% within the first week of the new site being live.

Is site speed really that important for conversions?

Yes. A large share of mobile visitors abandon a page that takes more than a few seconds to load, and each additional second of load time correlates with a measurable drop in conversions. Sub-1 second load time is one of the first things to fix before touching copy or design.

Should I rebuild my whole site or just run A/B tests on the current one?

If your current site is fast, mobile-first, and structurally sound, targeted testing is the right move. If the foundation itself is the problem — slow load times, a template that wasn't built for your business, no clear CTA — testing individual elements won't fix it. At that point a rebuild is usually faster and cheaper than months of incremental tests on a broken base.

What's the single highest-impact CRO fix?

There isn't one universal answer, but load speed and a single clear call to action consistently move the needle the most because they remove friction for every visitor, not just a segment. Fix those two before anything else.

How many form fields should a lead form have?

As few as you can get away with. Every field you ask for is a reason to abandon. Start with name, email, and maybe one qualifying question — you can gather the rest after the lead converts, in a follow-up call or email sequence.
